YT Studio

It is ticking

The newly released game in Steam. The game is about interacting with components to defuse the bomb. It also features a workshop so that user can freely create their own bomb and upload it so that everyone can play. Developing this game was a formidable challenge. The intricate game mechanics demanded meticulous handling of race conditions, time complexity so that even player make a complicated bomb, the game logic still runs correctly and smoothly.
But still, this challenge made me feel excited and began to make this game come true. I started off by figuring out what to do with the workshop editor. Given that the game's premise involves allowing players to create their own unique bombs, it was important that the editor be accessible and intuitive. Drawing inspiration from Oxygen Not Included, I crafted a 2D tile editor complete with input and output port mechanisms. User can drag and drop components, wires and run the gameplay to see if their creation works as expected.
Within the engine component, the wire system employs multi-threading in conjunction with a Breadth-First Search (BFS) algorithm to efficiently navigate and update the wire map whenever changes occur. To guarantee the game's smooth functionality, even in scenarios where users create looped motherboards or encounter race conditions, I introduced a trace-back system. This feature acts as a fail-safe, ensuring that the game can continue to operate without disruptions, even when users push the boundaries of its mechanics. Besides that, I also did:

  1. Design and implement UI
  2. VFX and SFX effects.
  3. Design bomb.
This game presented the exact challenge I was seeking. Completing it not only bolstered my self-confidence but also served as a catalyst, inspiring me to delve into the creation of even more intricate and complex mechanic-driven games.

The last barrier

Following the successful completion of "It is Ticking," our team wasted no time and embarked on a new game project, aptly titled "The Last Barrier." This game unfolds in a post-apocalyptic world marred by a rampant zombification epidemic, where humanity has been brought to its knees by a mysterious disease that transforms individuals into monstrous creatures. However, hope persists as humans are now engaged in a relentless battle for survival. They discovered of the vaccine which allowed them to resist the virus, and now they're determined to mount a counteroffensive against the infected hordes and reclaim their world. Following the main character, our hero, alongside his loyal comrades, will confront numerous perilous threats. Enhance their arsenal, bolster their team's capabilities, and become the vanguard of humanity's resurgence on this arduous journey. The game will soon released in GooglePlay Store and IOS store.
Developing a game with thousands of monsters is no small feat, especially when optimizing it to run smoothly on mobile devices. Fortunately, Unity now offers support for the "Entity-Component System" (ECS) along with the Job System and Burst Compiler. These tools are invaluable for optimizing game memory layout, ensuring the safety of multi-threading within the game system, and significantly improving cache hit rates. Additionally, Unity's built-in culling system effectively halts the updating of animations when the renderer is distant from the camera. All of these features combined contribute to our game's performance, making it not only feasible but also enjoyable to run on mid-level phones such as the Samsung A-3x.

In our game, the relentless hordes of monsters tend to arrive in large groups, requiring a well-thought-out strategy for optimizing. I've adopted a "divide and conquer" approach, where I segregate the monsters into distinct groups. Each group is led by a designated leader responsible for conducting collider casts or raycasts to assess group conditions. The group will perform AI tree decision and movement calculation for all monsters in that group. The results of these calculations are synchronized and enforced after a 2-frame delay. This strategy minimizes the computational burden on the main thread, allowing it to primarily focus on dispatching messages to the monsters for animation changes.
* The "divide and conquer" approach also proves advantageous in avoiding the overhead associated with resizing, inserting, or deleting elements from list in C# (vector in C++). This is particularly crucial because groups closest to the main character are more likely to experience changes their members . By managing monsters in separate groups, we minimize the need for frequent modifications to lists, resulting in more efficient memory management and improved overall performance.

Furthermore, considering that numerous monsters may have multiple attacks, the simultaneous execution of all these attacks in the traditional approach would lead to a substantial number of updates that need to be processed. To address this challenge, I've adopted a strategy reminiscent of Age of Empires 2. In this strategy, I've separated the attack data from the animation process. The attack frames data are executed on a separate thread, which allows for more efficient handling of multiple attacks occurring simultaneously. When necessary, collider casting is dispatched to accurately detect targets for these attacks. This approach optimizes the game's performance and ensures that combat remains smooth and responsive even when dealing with a large number of attacking monsters.

By separating the attack data from the animation process, I've eliminated the need for animation events to spawn projectiles or perform time-based box casting for melee attacks. As a result, animations are primarily used for visualization purposes. Leveraging animation culling techniques, I ensure that animations outside of the camera frustum (the box used by the camera in the rendering pipeline) do not run. This optimization conserves valuable computing power, contributing to the game's overall performance efficiency.

For now, the game is almost completed, I was so happy to see the game run smoothly in my tested device. Hope to see the game in the marketplace soon.

Hyper casual games

This is where our studio's journey began. While our team was brimming with enthusiasm, we initially faced the challenge of not knowing where to begin. I often found myself in the role of a direct mentor, providing detailed instructions and guidance. It was during this phase that we decided to venture into the hypercasual genre. In this genre, we embarked on the creation of several games, a total of six. While some of these games proved to be successful, generating profit and contributing to our growth, others did not perform as expected. Nevertheless, witnessing our projects generate revenue served as a powerful motivator for the team, igniting a collective desire to tackle even more ambitious endeavors in the future. This phase marked the foundation of our studio's experience and paved the way for the pursuit of larger, more impactful projects.
